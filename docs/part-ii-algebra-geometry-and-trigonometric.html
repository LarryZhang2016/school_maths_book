0.2 Solving equations with two unknowns

Let’s try to solve the this problem:

Alan thought two numbers and he asked Alice to guess what they are. Alan told Alice that the sum of the two numbers is 100 and the difference between the two numbers is 36. Can you help Alice figure out the two numbers?

Analysis: We must find the two numbers, which are unknowns to us. So, why not let the two unknowns be \(x\) and \(y\). Immediately, we have the following two equations \[ \left\{ \begin{array}{ccl} x + y &=& 100\\ x - y &=& 36 \end{array} \right. \] Now, if we can solve the two equations then we solve the problem.

This chapter is about solving equations with two unknowns. By that, I mean we want to find \(x\) and \(y\) in equations like the above one. The method for solving this type of equations is called the elimination method; as the name indicates the key is that we firstly knock out one unknown and then we can get the other one.

For the above the problem, we can knock out \(y\) be adding the two equations together \[ 2x = 136, \] from which easily we have \(x=\frac{136}{2}=68\). Next, to find \(y\), we can either replace 68 for \(x\) in one equation to have \(68+y=100\), which yields \(y=32\), or this time we knock out \(y\) by using the top equation to subtract the second equation \[ 2y = 64, \] from which again we get \(y=32\).

Let’s have another problem.

Solve the following equations: \[\begin{align} 2x + 3y & = 6 \tag{1}\\ x + y & = 2 \tag{2} \end{align}\] Solution: We can use the elimination method. In order to knock out \(x\), we multiply 2 on both sides of Eq. (2), and we have \[\begin{equation} 2x + 2y = 4 \tag{3} \end{equation}\]

The result of Eq. (1) and Eq. (3) is \[ y=2. \] Plugging \(y=2\) into (2). we have \[ x + 2 = 2, \] from which we get \(x=0\). Final answer: \(x=0\) and \(y=2.\)

Our third problem: Solve the following equations \[\begin{align} y &= \frac{x}{2} \tag{4}\\ x + 3y &= 10 \tag{5} \end{align}\]

We provide two solutions for the problem. The essential spirits in the two solutions are the same—we must firstly eliminate one unknown in order to get the other unknown.

Solution 1: Since Eq. (4) says \(y\) is \(x/2\), we can replace \(y\) by \(x/2\) in Eq. (5) and we have \[ x + 3\times \frac{1}{2}\times x = 10, \] which can be simplified to \[\begin{equation} \frac{5}{2}x = 10 \tag{6} \end{equation}\]

Multiplying \(2/5\) on both sides of (6) yields \(x=4\). \(y=x/2=4/2=2\). Final answer: \(x=4\) and \(y=2\).

Solution 2: We firstly change (4) to \[\begin{equation} \frac{1}{2}x - y = 0 \tag{7} \end{equation}\]

Eq. (7)\(\times 3\) + (5) is \[ \frac{5}{2}x = 10, \] from which we have \(x=4\). \(y=x/2=4/2=2\). Final answer: \(x=4\) and \(y=2\).
